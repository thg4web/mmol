Answers to common questions about the Messier Marathon, the Astronomical League Messier Observing Program, and how to get the most from this log.

The Messier Marathon

The Messier Marathon is a challenge to observe all 110 Messier objects in a single night. It is best attempted in late March, when the geometry of the sky from mid-northern latitudes allows every object to be above the horizon at some point between dusk and dawn.

Objects are observed in a specific west-to-east sequence, beginning with those that set first just after sunset, and finishing with those that rise last just before sunrise. The # column in this log reflects that optimal search order.

The ideal window is the few nights around new moon in late March, typically between March 18 and April 5 for most mid-northern latitudes (roughly 25°–50° N). The new moon is essential: even a thin crescent can wash out faint objects low on the horizon at the start and end of the night.

The earlier in the window you observe, the better your chances with the difficult western objects (M74, M77) at dusk. The later, the more time you have for M30 and other eastern objects at dawn. Most experienced marathoners aim for the first or second night after new moon in the last week of March.

Difficulty varies enormously across the catalog. Showpiece objects like M42 (Orion Nebula), M45 (Pleiades), and M31 (Andromeda Galaxy) are naked-eye targets, while objects like M74, M76, M91, and M98 are notoriously faint or have very low surface brightness, requiring genuinely dark skies and patient averted vision.

During a marathon, the hardest objects are those at the extremes of the night, M74 and M77 in the west at dusk, and M30 in the east just before dawn, where the sky is not fully dark or is already brightening, and the targets sit low on the horizon in thick atmosphere. The Size column in the log gives the angular dimensions of each object, a useful guide to expected difficulty: small, faint objects at low surface brightness are typically the hardest to confirm at low altitude.

Most Messier objects are within reach of a 4-inch (101mm) telescope under extremely good conditions. We do not recommend using anything under a 6–8 in. (152–203mm) telescope. More aperture will make the night considerably more comfortable, especially for the fainter galaxies in Virgo and the small planetary nebulae.

A low-power wide-field eyepiece is invaluable for sweeping between objects quickly. A red flashlight and a printed or digital finder chart are essential. The Astronomical League's rules specifically prohibit GoTo telescopes, computerised setting circles, and phone apps that point to objects, you are expected to navigate the sky yourself using a finderscope or Telrad.

You need a site with a clear, unobstructed horizon in all directions, particularly to the west (for the early evening objects) and to the east and southeast (for the Sagittarius objects and M30 before dawn). Even a small hill or tree line in the wrong direction can cost you several objects.

Dark skies are critical. Light pollution washes out low-surface-brightness galaxies in Virgo and Coma Berenices and brightens the horizon at dusk and dawn when the hardest targets are lowest. A Bortle 4 or darker site gives you the best chance of a complete run.

Astronomical League Messier Observing Program

The Astronomical League Messier Observing Program is a formal certification for amateur astronomers who observe and log Messier objects. There are two certification levels:

Silver Level — observe any 70 Messier objects and submit a qualifying log. Earns a certificate.

Gold Level — observe all 110 Messier objects and submit a complete log. Earns a certificate and an AL Messier pin, signed by the current AL President.

Certification is open to AL members, either through a local astronomical society or as members at large.

For each object your log must record: date and time, latitude and longitude of your observing site, seeing and transparency conditions, telescope aperture, magnification used, and a written description of the object as it appears through the eyepiece.

This log captures all of those fields in the full AL observation record form (click the ✎ button on any row). Use ☰ Log → Session Setup to auto-fill location and equipment into every record you open each night. When ready to submit, use ☰ Log → Generate AL Report to produce a formatted multi-page PDF suitable for submission to your AL club coordinator.

Acceptable aids include finderscopes, Telrads, and similar non-electronic finders. The Stellarium Web link in the object lightbox is useful for planning and identifying what a star field should look like, but the actual object must be found by eye at the eyepiece.

The Messier Marathon is a separate personal challenge that aligns with the certification, but the program is designed to be completed at your own pace through all four seasons.

Many observers work through the catalog systematically by constellation or season, which is a thoroughly rewarding way to build deep-sky experience regardless of whether a marathon attempt is part of your plans.

By default the table is sorted in Marathon order — the Don Machholz sequence from #1 (objects that set first after dusk) to #110 (objects that rise last before dawn). This is the order to use during a marathon night.

Clicking the Object column header toggles to Numeric order (M1 through M110 by Messier number), useful for cross-referencing with books, charts, and the AL certificate. Click the header again to return to Marathon order. All filters, search, and the lightbox work correctly in both modes.

The filter buttons (All, Galaxy, Open Cluster, Globular, Nebula, Unobserved) narrow the table to objects of that type, or to objects you haven't checked off yet. The Unobserved filter is particularly useful mid-session to see only what remains.

The search box filters by any text in the table, M-number, constellation abbreviation (e.g. "Sgr"), object type (e.g. "Globular"), or marathon order number. Search and filter can be combined, for example filter to Globular Cluster and type "Her" to show only Hercules globulars.

The Finder Chart button opens a local chart image from your charts/ folder (if present). A warning notice is shown above the button reminding you to enable your device's red filter before viewing, chart images have a white background and will harm your dark adaptation if opened without a filter at the eyepiece.

Stellarium Web (stellarium-web.org) opens a real-time 3D sky simulation centred on the selected object. Set your location and date/time in Stellarium for an accurate view of where the object sits in your sky tonight. Both links require an internet connection and open in a new tab.

The PSA column references page numbers in the Pocket Sky Atlas by Roger Sinnott, published by Sky & Telescope, one of the most widely used printed star atlases among amateur astronomers. The page references let you quickly jump to the right chart in the atlas for each object during a session.

The human eye's rod cells, responsible for low-light vision, are not sensitive to red wavelengths. White or blue light bleaches the rhodopsin in the rods, and full dark adaptation takes 20–30 minutes to recover. Red light lets you read screens and charts at the eyepiece without resetting that process.

This log's night mode, deep black background, red-spectrum text, ASCII labels replacing all bright emoji icons, and red-filtered lightbox photos, is designed to be used directly at the telescope. The day mode (warm parchment) is for planning and review under normal lighting.
